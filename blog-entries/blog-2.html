Week 2 (9/10/21): Negative Space

What is Negative Space?

Claimed to be one of the fundamental building blocks to design, "negative space", also commonly known as "white space", is the space provided between elements in a composition. Negative space is a section of a webpage left completely empty, including margins and gutters in the surrounding blank exterior of a page.

Typically, webpages following the box model exhibit some use of negative space:

What does Negative Space Mean to the Viewer?

As a web designer, you may be questioning the importance of negative space. Isn't this empty space a total waste of real estate, where more information, images, or other elements could fit? The point of negative space is to give the viewer some visual breathing room, making it easy on the eyes. Moreover, there is a greater feeling of balance to the webpage. With limited space, the viewer may feel overwhelmed getting loads of information thrown at them all at once on a single screen. In general, content is consumed better vertically.

While the use of negative space isn't a requirement for building a webpage, it should be a forethought in keeping a designer's audience engaged. This leads us into minimalist web design. Often times "less is more." A user takes about a second or less to form an opinion about a webpage, and if it looks cluttered, it will drive their attention away. The concept of negative space enhances the user experience of a webpage.

At a very basic level, applying some extra space to the margins and giving the page some padding to the content lessens the user tracing back and forth across the screen of a webpage, keeping the user focused on the page:
